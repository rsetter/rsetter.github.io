Dense urban coastal areas face unique adaptation challenges that differ fundamentally from rural or low-density coastlines. Using Waikiki — a proxy for high-value, tourism-dependent coastal economies globally — this study estimates the costs and tradeoffs of four managed retreat and adaptation pathways under three sea level rise scenarios. Contrary to previous findings where infrastructure dominated adaptation costs, we demonstrate that in dense, high-rise tourism hubs, the cost of building-level adaptation is the primary economic driver, representing a paradigm shift for coastal urban planning. Findings from Waikiki offer a window into the future challenges facing places like Miami, Hong Kong, and the Gold Coast.
